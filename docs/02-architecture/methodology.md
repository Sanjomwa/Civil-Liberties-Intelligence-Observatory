# CLIO Methodology

CLIO (Civil Liberties Intelligence Observatory) turns noisy, independently-collected signals about civil-liberties pressure into attributed, confidence-qualified findings. This document states the method in two parts: first as a general discipline for fusing independent evidence into guarded conclusions, independent of any particular data source or domain; then as it is actually implemented today, using two real evidence channels — internet-censorship measurement and conflict-event reporting — as a worked example.

A note on scope before the method itself: CLIO does not claim uniform rigor across every part of its pipeline, and it does not claim predictive or causal power. Both limits are stated explicitly in the closing section, because stating them is itself part of the method, not an afterthought.

## Part 1 — The method, independent of any particular evidence source

This is the actual discipline being followed, stated at a level of abstraction where it stops being "how one particular pipeline works" and becomes a general architecture for fusing independent, imperfect evidence into confidence-qualified conclusions. Read literally: if every reference to a specific data source or technology were removed from this part, it would still describe a complete method on its own. That is the test this section is written to pass.

### Admissibility criteria — deciding what counts as evidence at all

Before a raw signal is treated as an "observation" in the sense below, it is checked against criteria along six dimensions. This is a deliberate, stated discipline for deciding whether something has earned the right to be treated as evidence, not a formality applied after the fact:

- **Provenance.** Does the signal's declared origin actually match how it was collected? A system whose documentation names one source while its real collection mechanism reads from another has a provenance problem regardless of how plausible the resulting number looks — and this kind of quiet divergence is one of the more damaging failures available to a system like this, because nothing about the eventual output signals that it happened.
- **Legality.** Is the right to collect, hold, and use this signal for this purpose actually established, rather than assumed from convenience or the absence of an objection?
- **Attribution.** Can the signal be traced back to the specific channel that produced it? A composite conclusion can only be broken back down into its contributing channels later if that traceability exists from the start.
- **Reproducibility.** Could the signal, in principle, be regenerated — by re-running the same collection method, or by an independent party applying the declared method to the same raw input? A signal that cannot in principle be reproduced by anyone but its original collector is only admissible with that limitation stated plainly, never silently.
- **Temporal integrity.** Is the signal's timestamp trustworthy, and is later analysis prevented from treating a signal as though it had been available earlier than it actually was? This guards against a conclusion about a given period quietly incorporating information that only arrived afterward.
- **Grain declaration.** Is the signal's true resolution — temporal, entity-level, or otherwise — stated honestly, rather than left to be inferred later by whichever downstream step first needs it?

These six criteria are not applied uniformly by a single mechanical gate: some are enforced automatically in code, one is a human-reviewed evaluation process applied before a new evidence source is ever adopted, and — critically — a signal failing reproducibility is not necessarily excluded outright. It may instead be admitted with the failure disclosed and structurally walled off from every score it would otherwise feed. "Not yet reproducible, and excluded from scoring as a result" is treated as an honest, first-class outcome, not a defect to hide.

### Stage 1 — Independent observation

A raw signal is captured with zero interpretive content attached: no "blocked," no "escalating," no directional judgment of any kind — only what was actually measured or reported. This discipline further requires drawing on **at least two structurally independent observation channels**, not two views or exports of the same underlying channel, because agreement between genuinely independent channels is what makes a later conclusion attributable rather than merely repeated. A system with a single observation channel, however well-instrumented, can observe; it cannot yet practice this method.

### Stage 2 — Confidence-qualified interpretation

Every classification applied to an observation is produced by explicit, inspectable rules rather than an ad hoc judgment call, and it carries its own separately-computed confidence value, derived from evidence density, sample size, and variance. Confidence is a first-class, continuous quantity computed alongside the classification — never a bare label asserted without one, and never conflated with the classification itself. "What was this observation classified as" and "how sure is the system of that classification" are two different questions, answered by two different mechanisms. A corollary: converting a continuous confidence value into a small number of named tiers for display must be governed by one shared reference, not re-implemented independently at each place a threshold is needed — otherwise the same measurement could be labeled differently depending on which part of the system reads it.

### Stage 3 — Guarded inference

A directional conclusion — any label implying escalation, stability, severity, or a trend — is permitted only after explicit statistical guardrails clear: sufficient evidence density for the period or entity in question, non-degenerate variance (a flat signal can mean genuine stability, or it can mean the instrument isn't sensitive to what's actually happening — these are different things and must be distinguishable), and no grain violation (see below). Critically, **"insufficient evidence" must be a first-class, nameable output state** — not a fallback, not an error, not silently defaulted to the nearest confident-sounding label. A method that cannot say "we don't know" as a real, distinguishable answer has not actually implemented guarded inference, no matter how sophisticated its confidence scoring looks.

### A continuous discipline underneath every stage: grain-honesty

A value computed at one resolution — weekly, semiannual, a lifetime summary for one entity with no time dimension at all, or any other grain — must never be presented, joined, or interpreted as though it had a finer resolution than it actually has. This is not a stage of its own; it is a constraint every stage above must obey continuously, from the moment a signal is captured through to final presentation. Violating it is one of the more consequential and easy-to-miss failure modes available to a system like this, precisely because a value that has been silently broadcast to a finer grain than it earned can look, superficially, exactly like a value that legitimately has that resolution.

### Extension A — Attributable inference

Where multiple independent, confidence-qualified interpretations are fused into a single composite conclusion, that composite should be decomposable back into its named, confidence-qualified component drivers on demand — not delivered only as one opaque number. A reader should be able to ask "why is this conclusion what it is" and get an answer naming which independent channels contributed, how much, and at what confidence, with any corroborating signal that isn't itself an arithmetic component of the composite clearly labeled as corroboration rather than silently folded into the fused number.

### Extension B — Source admission

A candidate observation channel is not admitted merely because it would be methodologically useful. It is evaluated in order: first, whether its licensing, legal, and commercial terms are compatible with how the system's outputs will actually be used — evaluated *before* any methodological feasibility work begins, not after; second, on methodological grounds, against two distinct, non-substitutable questions: does the candidate **strengthen an existing relationship** between two other channels into something newly attributable, or does it **open a decision-relevant question no existing channel asks at all**? A system that only asks the first question will systematically undervalue real new evidence that only does the second.

## Part 2 — The method in practice: internet-censorship measurement and conflict-event reporting

Everything below is one implementation of Part 1's disciplines, in one domain — measuring civil-liberties pressure in Kenya — using two primary evidence channels. Nothing here redefines Part 1; where something below looks like a new rule, it is Part 1's existing discipline applied to this domain's specifics.

A few terms used throughout, defined plainly:

- **OONI** (Open Observatory of Network Interference) is an independent measurement network that runs automated network probes from real vantage points to detect internet censorship — whether a given site or service is reachable, and if not, at which stage of the connection (DNS resolution, a raw TCP connection, a TLS handshake, or an HTTP request) it failed.
- **ACLED** (Armed Conflict Location & Event Data Project) is an independent research organization that codes individual conflict, protest, and political-violence events from on-the-ground and media sourcing, at the level of specific dates, locations, and event types.
- **ASN** (Autonomous System Number) identifies a specific network operator on the internet — in this context, typically the internet service provider whose infrastructure a censorship measurement passed through or was blocked on.
- A **regime classifier**, as used below, is a component that assigns a period of time a categorical label describing the overall state of a tracked condition (for example, "stable" versus "in crisis"), based on statistical thresholds applied to underlying signals, rather than a single measurement read in isolation.
- CLIO's own internal pipeline moves data through layered stages, each a stricter refinement of the one before: a **staging** layer holds cleaned, source-native data; an **intermediate** layer applies classification logic; a **features** layer computes rolling statistics and guardrail flags; an **intelligence** layer performs regime and relationship inference; and **marts** and **reporting** layers hold analytics-ready, decomposable outputs meant for direct consumption.

### Admissibility, in practice

Every signal CLIO admits into its observation layer is expected to clear the six admissibility criteria described above before it is treated as evidence, and grain-honesty is applied continuously to every one of them, not just once at intake. Concretely, as implemented today:

- **Provenance** is verified by direct inspection: each staging asset's declared upstream source is checked against what its own extraction code actually reads, rather than trusted from its own documentation. Where a mismatch like this has been found historically, it has been corrected — the current, reconfirmed state is that declared and actual provenance agree across CLIO's live evidence channels.
- **Legality** is evaluated by a single, dedicated evaluation process applied to every candidate evidence channel before it is adopted — an explicit, multi-dimension scoring framework that CLIO applies uniformly, covering independence, methodological transparency, licensing and commercial-use compatibility, and long-term access risk, among other factors. This is a human-reviewed evaluation gate, not a continuously-running code check, since adopting a new evidence source is an infrequent, deliberated decision rather than a per-signal event.
- **Attribution** is maintained structurally rather than by a per-row tag: each evidence channel has its own dedicated pipeline, from intake through to CLIO's composite and attribution views, and every value that reaches a shared, cross-channel output is carried under a channel-specific, explicitly-named column the entire way — never merged into an undifferentiated shared field. A reader of any composite output can trace a given number back to the named channel it came from.
- **Reproducibility** is not a uniform pass across every evidence category, and CLIO discloses that plainly rather than asserting otherwise. One evidence category — legal and platform-takedown pressure at individual-request grain — does not yet have a production-quality, regenerable source behind it. Rather than silently omit that category or quietly fill it with unflagged data, CLIO keeps its schema and disclosure machinery in place, tags it with a real, per-record flag, structurally excludes it from every score it would otherwise feed, and documents the exclusion in its published methodology material. This is a deliberate, disclosed absence, not a silently-missing one — guarded inference's "insufficient evidence is a first-class output" discipline, applied at the level of an entire evidence category rather than a single classification.
- **Temporal integrity** is enforced concretely for CLIO's stateful regime classifier (see below): an execution-time precondition check verifies that a run touches exactly one entity and one new time period before it is allowed to proceed, specifically to prevent a run from silently corrupting or double-counting already-committed history.
- **Grain declaration** is stated explicitly for CLIO's two primary channels — each carries an explicit statement, at the point its cleaned data lands, of its true native resolution, and for the conflict-event channel that declaration is itself a checked, enforced schema field rather than a comment. It is not yet uniformly explicit at intake for every secondary channel; where a slower-moving channel's true period-based grain isn't declared as early as it could be, CLIO's continuous grain-honesty discipline still catches and correctly handles it at every later join and presentation step, as described below — declaration at intake and continuous enforcement are related but distinct safeguards, and CLIO relies more heavily on the second than the first today.

### Independent observation, in practice

CLIO's two primary evidence channels — internet-censorship measurement (OONI) and conflict-event reporting (ACLED) — are structurally independent: different collection methods, different organizations, different failure modes, measuring different aspects of the same underlying phenomenon. Neither is a view or export of the other. OONI's measurements cover DNS, TCP, TLS, and HTTP layer results across six specific test types targeting particular services, rather than a single generic connectivity check. ACLED's records are individual, dated, located conflict and protest events. A third, lower-weight channel — government platform-takedown request data, published by Google's Transparency Report — provides a slower-moving, coarser-grain observation of formal legal pressure on platforms, used as context rather than as a source of incident-level claims (see grain-honesty below).

### Confidence-qualified interpretation, in practice

Every classification CLIO produces, on both primary channels, is generated by explicit rules rather than ad hoc judgment, and every classification carries its own separately-computed confidence value. On the censorship-measurement side, each individual measurement is assigned a specific blocking classification (for example, distinguishing a DNS-level block from a TLS handshake failure) together with a confidence score, and that continuous score is converted into a small number of named confidence tiers via a single shared reference table used consistently everywhere a tier is needed — not re-implemented independently at each consuming site. On the conflict-event side, CLIO goes further than a single confidence axis: each event is assigned two independent quantities — how confident the classifier is in this specific label, and, separately, how comparable this category of event is to classify consistently in the first place. Treating "how sure" and "how comparable" as two different questions, each with its own answer, is a more complete model than a single confidence axis would allow.

### Guarded inference, in practice

CLIO evaluates explicit statistical guardrails — evidence density, signal variance, and grain violations — before assigning any directional conclusion, and treats "insufficient evidence" as a first-class output on both primary channels, not a fallback.

The conflict-event channel's inference layer is additionally **stateful**: it is a regime classifier that reads its own prior period's committed result as an input to the current one, so that a regime, once established, persists appropriately rather than flickering week to week on noise alone. Any classifier built this way needs an enforced precondition that a run touches exactly one entity and one new time period at a time — otherwise a re-run or overlapping execution can silently corrupt persisted state in a way that produces no visible error. CLIO's implementation enforces this precondition directly: a run is refused outright, before any state is written, if it would violate it.

This classifier assigns each week one of several possible regime states, ranging from a stable baseline through escalating categories to its most severe state. Validated against the full available history for Kenya, it has correctly identified escalation and reached its most severe classification during real, independently-documented periods of unrest, and — just as importantly — has correctly returned a stable reading at known peaceful reference points, rather than defaulting to alarm. A true negative is as important a validation result as a true positive, and CLIO treats it that way.

A word of caution on terminology: several different classification scales exist across CLIO's pipeline, at different layers, for different purposes, and more than one of them happens to use the word "elevated" (or a close variant) as a category label. These are not the same scale, do not share thresholds, and are not interchangeable. Any published figure using a word like this should always state precisely which scale it refers to — CLIO does not use it as a bare, unqualified term.

### Attributable inference, in practice

CLIO maintains a dedicated pressure-attribution view that decomposes its cross-source conflict-and-platform pressure score into its named component drivers — a conflict-intensity term and a platform-takedown-pressure term — each shown with its actual weight, its contribution to the composite, and its true underlying temporal grain, so that day-to-day or period-to-period movement can be attributed to the correct term rather than assumed. Network-measurement evidence is not an arithmetic input to this particular composite at all; it is shown alongside it, on its own view, clearly labeled as independent corroboration rather than folded into the fused number. This decomposition — naming exactly which channel drove a given number, by how much, and at what grain — is what lets a reader ask "why is this score what it is" and get a real answer, on this view, rather than an opaque figure.

**A disclosed methodological tension CLIO keeps visible rather than smooths over, because it is a strength, not a weakness:** CLIO's categorical (event-based) and continuous (composite-score) inference layers can and do disagree during the same real incident, and CLIO reports both rather than only the number that tells the cleaner story. During Kenya's Finance Bill 2024 unrest, the categorical classifier reached its most severe classification the week Parliament was stormed, while the continuous composite score's reading for that same week — while clearly above its own recent baseline — did not reach the composite's own most severe band. Disclosing that two independently-computed inference layers can genuinely disagree, rather than reporting only whichever one tells the more dramatic story, is itself evidence of methodological honesty.

**On grain-honesty specifically, stated precisely because it is CLIO's most reputationally important claim:** the platform-takedown channel used above publishes at a roughly six-month cadence. On its own dedicated attribution view, it is shown only at that true, native period grain — never claimed at finer resolution than it has. Inside the composite score's day-by-day arithmetic, however, that same period value is necessarily held constant across every day until the channel's next real update — meaning the composite is a step function with respect to that term specifically, moving only when the faster-updating conflict-event term moves, for as much as six months at a stretch. These are two different, both-true statements about the same input, not a contradiction: native-grain display on the attribution view, and constant-carry-forward inside continuously-updating arithmetic between refreshes. Stating both, rather than only the first, is what grain-honesty actually requires here.

### Source admission, in practice

A candidate evidence channel is admitted to CLIO only after its licensing and commercial terms are confirmed compatible with CLIO's intended use — checked first, before methodological work begins — and then evaluated for whether it strengthens an existing relationship between two channels into something newly attributable, or opens a decision-relevant question no existing channel asks at all.

As of this writing: CLIO's two primary channels are treated as foundation-tier and non-commercial, pending a separate commercial licensing arrangement with either source. A third channel has been evaluated and cleared for commercial use, but is not yet ingested into the pipeline. A fourth — the platform-takedown channel described above — operates at a coarser, semiannual grain that cannot honestly support incident-level interpretation, so CLIO deliberately feeds it only at that real grain; its own reuse and redistribution terms remain a separate, still-unresolved question, distinct from its already-settled grain classification. One further candidate organization is, on inspection, actually two separate datasets with two separate evaluation outcomes, and CLIO is careful not to conflate them: one is not currently usable pending resolution of a licensing conflict, and the other — a different product from the same organization, intended only as secondary, corroborating evidence rather than a primary channel — has not been ingested either, and remains under the same licensing review as the first. Neither is currently part of CLIO's evidence base.

### A disclosed integrity gap, not a methodology gap

CLIO does not yet have a production-quality source for legal and platform-takedown pressure at individual-request grain. Rather than silently omitting that evidence category, or quietly filling it with an unflagged placeholder, CLIO keeps the category's schema and disclosure machinery fully in place, structurally excludes it from every score it would otherwise feed, and states the gap plainly in its published methodology material. This is guarded inference's "insufficient evidence is a first-class output" discipline, applied at the level of an entire evidence category rather than one classification — a disclosed absence, not a hidden one.

## What CLIO does not claim

CLIO does not claim its evidentiary rigor is uniform across every source and subsystem it fuses today — some parts of the pipeline are more methodologically mature than others, and this document has tried to say precisely where. CLIO also does not claim predictive or causal power: its evidence is retrospective and confidence-qualified, deliberately not designed or positioned to forecast future events or assert causation. Stating both limits explicitly, rather than leaving them implicit, is itself part of the method.
